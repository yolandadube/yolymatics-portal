Introduction

What Are Wave Behaviours?

Waves transfer energy through a medium without transporting matter. Understanding wave behaviour means understanding how they reflect, refract, diffract, and interfere.

Section 01

Reflection of Waves

Law of Reflection

The angle of incidence always equals the angle of reflection, both measured from the normal to the surface.

\[ \theta_i = \theta_r \]

💡 Mirror Thought Experiment

Looking directly at a mirror: θᵢ = 0°. The ray travels along the normal, strikes the mirror, and returns along the same path. θᵣ = 0° also.

Section 02

Refraction & Speed Change

When a wave crosses a boundary between two media of different densities, it changes speed and consequently bends (refracts).

Snell's Law

\[ n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2 \]

where \(n = c/v\) is the refractive index.

🔑 Entering denser medium (n₂ > n₁): wave slows → bends toward the normal (θ₂ < θ₁).

Entering less dense: wave speeds → bends away from normal.

\[ n = \frac{c}{v} \qquad \lambda_2 = \frac{\lambda_1 n_1}{n_2} \]
Section 03

Diffraction — Bending Around Obstacles

Diffraction is the spreading of waves after passing through an opening or around an obstacle. Significant diffraction occurs when the wavelength is comparable to the gap size.

\[ \text{Significant diffraction when:} \quad \lambda \approx d \]

d = slit / gap width

Key Rules

  • 📈 Larger λ → more diffraction
  • 📉 Smaller λ → less diffraction
  • 🔊 Sound (λ ≈ 0.1–10 m) diffracts easily around walls
  • 💡 Visible light (λ ≈ 400–700 nm) needs very narrow slits

🎵 You hear someone around a corner because sound diffracts — light does not, so you can't see them.

Section 04

Wave Interference

When two waves overlap, their displacements add algebraically at every point (principle of superposition).

✅ Constructive Interference

Crests align → amplitude doubles

❌ Destructive Interference

Crest meets trough → cancellation

Principle of Superposition

At any point, the resultant displacement is the algebraic sum of all individual wave displacements.

\[ y_{\text{total}} = y_1 + y_2 \]

Important Notes

  • • Amplitudes need NOT be equal for interference to occur
  • • After passing through, each wave continues unchanged
  • • Applies to light, sound, water waves, EM waves

Real Examples

Young's double slitNoise-cancelling headphonesThin-film colour

Section 05

Beats & Standing Waves

When two waves with nearly equal frequencies superpose, the amplitude periodically rises and falls — creating a beat.

\[ f_{\text{beat}} = |f_2 - f_1| \]

Standing Waves

The beat produces a standing wave pattern with fixed nodes (zero amplitude) and antinodes (maximum amplitude).

🎵 Real Applications

  • 🎸 Tuning instruments — hear the beat, adjust until it disappears
  • ✈️ Multi-engine aircraft synchronise throttles to eliminate beat
  • 🔧 Tuning forks provide a precise reference frequency

440 Hz and 441 Hz played together →

\[ f_{\text{beat}} = |441 - 440| = 1 \text{ Hz} \]

One "pulse" in amplitude per second

Section 06

The Doppler Effect

An apparent shift in frequency heard by an observer when the wave source and observer are in relative motion.

Doppler Formula (Sound)

\[ f_{\text{obs}} = f_s \left(\frac{v \pm v_o}{v \mp v_s}\right) \]

Use +v_o / −v_s when approaching;
−v_o / +v_s when receding.

What the diagram shows

Each circle is a wavefront emitted when the source was at an earlier position. As the source moves right, wavefronts ahead are compressed (higher f) and behind are stretched (lower f).

Applications

🏥 Medical ultrasound 🔭 Galaxy recession 🚗 Speed radar 🌦️ Weather radar
