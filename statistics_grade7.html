Part 1 — Mean
📊 The Mean (Average)
The mean is the fair share value — what each person would get if the total was shared equally.
Mean = Sum of all values ÷ Number of values

Steps to Find the Mean

  1. Add up ALL the values in the data set
  2. Count how many values there are
  3. Divide the total by the count
  4. Round if needed and include units

Important Notes

  • Mean works with decimals, fractions & negatives
  • The mean may not be a value in the data set
  • Extreme values (outliers) can shift the mean
  • Always check units in real-world problems
Part 1 — Mean Worked Example
📐 Mean — Worked Example

🌡️ Example: Weekly Temperatures in Cape Town (°C)

The maximum temperatures recorded over 7 days were:

Mon: 18.5   Tue: 21.0   Wed: 19.8   Thu: 23.4   Fri: 17.6   Sat: 22.1   Sun: 20.5

Step 1 — Add all values:

18.5 + 21.0 + 19.8 + 23.4 + 17.6 + 22.1 + 20.5 = 142.9

Step 2 — Count the values: 7 days

Step 3 — Divide:

Mean = 142.9 ÷ 7 = 20.41°C
∴ The mean temperature for the week was 20.41°C

🏦 Example 2: Monthly Savings (with negatives!)

Savings over 5 months (negative = money spent): R350, −R120, R275.50, −R80, R430

Sum = 350 + (−120) + 275.50 + (−80) + 430 = 855.50
Mean = 855.50 ÷ 5 = R171.10 per month
∴ On average, R171.10 was saved per month

Part 2 — Median
📊 The Median (Middle Value)
The median is the middle value when data is arranged in order. It is not affected by extreme values.

ODD number of values

  1. Arrange data in ascending order
  2. Find the middle value: position = (n+1) ÷ 2
  3. That value IS the median
3   7   9   12   15
Median = 9 (3rd of 5)

EVEN number of values

  1. Arrange data in ascending order
  2. Find the two middle values
  3. Median = average of those two values
3   7   9   12   15   20
Median = (9+12)÷2 = 10.5
Part 2 — Median Worked Example
📐 Median — Worked Example

💰 Example 1 (ODD): Pocket Money of 9 Learners (R)

R45   R120   R75   R30   R90   R55   R110   R85   R60

Step 1 — Sort ascending:

R30   R45   R55   R60   R75   R85   R90   R110   R120

Step 2 — Middle position: (9+1) ÷ 2 = 5th value

∴ Median pocket money = R75

🏃 Example 2 (EVEN): Race times (minutes) of 10 athletes

12.5   14.8   11.2   16.0   13.4   9.75   15.3   12.8   10.5   14.1

Sorted:

9.75   10.5   11.2   12.5   12.8   13.4   14.1   14.8   15.3   16.0

Two middle values (5th and 6th): 12.8 and 13.4

Median = (12.8 + 13.4) ÷ 2 = 26.2 ÷ 2 = 13.1 minutes
∴ Median race time = 13.1 minutes

Part 3 — Mode & Frequency
📊 Mode & Frequency Tables
The mode is the value that appears most often in a data set.
A frequency table helps us count and organise how often each value appears.

    Key Notes

  • Data can have NO mode (all unique)
  • Data can have two modes (bimodal)
  • Mode works for non-numerical data too

Part 4 — Range
📏 The Range of a Data Set
The range tells us the spread of the data — the distance between the largest and smallest values.
Range = Maximum value − Minimum value

🌡️ Worked Example: Overnight Temperatures (°C)

−4.5    2.8    −1.2    6.5    0    3.7    −3.1

Maximum: 6.5°C     Minimum: −4.5°C

Range = 6.5 − (−4.5) = 6.5 + 4.5 = 11.0°C
∴ The temperatures varied by 11°C over the week

⚠️ Watch out! When subtracting a negative: a − (−b) = a + b

📉

Small Range

Values are close together → consistent, predictable data

📈

Large Range

Values are spread out → variable, unpredictable data

Part 5 — Line Plots
📍 Line Plots
A line plot shows data on a number line, with a dot or × above each value for each time it occurs. Great for small data sets!

How to Draw a Line Plot

  1. Draw a number line covering the data range
  2. Label the scale with equal intervals
  3. For each value, place a dot (●) or × above it
  4. Stack marks if a value appears more than once
  5. Add a title and axis label

What You Can Read

  • 🏆 Mode — tallest column of dots
  • 📏 Range — rightmost minus leftmost value
  • 📊 Distribution — where data clusters
  • 🔍 Outliers — isolated dots far from others
  • 🔢 Total count — count all dots

Part 6 — Stem & Leaf Plots
🌿 Stem & Leaf Plots
A stem & leaf plot organises data while keeping every original value. The stem = leading digit(s), the leaf = last digit.

How to Draw One

  1. Sort data from smallest to largest
  2. Write stems in a column (left side)
  3. Write leaves to the right of each stem
  4. Add a KEY showing what stem|leaf means

What You Can Read

  • 📊 Shape of the data distribution
  • 🔢 Exact original values are preserved
  • 🏆 Mode — leaf that appears most often
  • 📏 Range = largest leaf − smallest leaf (overall)
  • 📍 Median — count to the middle value
💡 Key notation:   4 | 3 8   means values 43 and 48
Part 6 — Stem & Leaf Worked Example
📐 Stem & Leaf — Worked Example

📝 Maths Test Scores for 15 Students (out of 100)

43   48   51   55   57   62   65   65   68   71   74   78   82   85   93
Stem│Leaves
4│3  8
5│1  5  7
6│2  5  5  8
7│1  4  8
8│2  5
9│3
Key: 4│3 means 43
📌 Mode = 65 (leaf 5 appears twice in stem 6)
📏 Range = 93 − 43 = 50
📍 Median = 8th value = 65
🔢 Total = 15 students
💡 Tip: Data is already sorted in a stem & leaf plot — the median is just the middle value (count carefully!)

Part 7 — Variability
📉 Variability — How Spread Out Is the Data?
Variability measures how spread out or clustered data is. Two sets can have the same mean but very different variability!

City A — LOW Variability 🏙️

18   19   20   21   22

Mean = 20°C    Range = 4°C

Temperatures are close together — very consistent and predictable

City B — HIGH Variability 🌪️

5   12   20   28   35

Mean = 20°C    Range = 30°C

Same mean — but temperatures are all over the place!

Conclusion 🔍

The range is a simple measure of variability:

  • 📉 Small range → low variability
  • 📈 Large range → high variability
  • Variability tells us how reliable the mean is
Part 7 — Variability
📐 Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD)
MAD tells us the average distance each value is from the mean. A higher MAD means more variability!
MAD = Sum of |value − mean| ÷ Number of values

📊 Worked Example: Quiz scores — 2, 4, 6, 8, 10

Step 1: Find the mean: (2+4+6+8+10) ÷ 5 = 30 ÷ 5 = 6

Step 2: Find absolute deviations:

Value|Value − Mean|Deviation
2|2 − 6|4
4|4 − 6|2
6|6 − 6|0
8|8 − 6|2
10|10 − 6|4
Total12

Step 3: MAD = 12 ÷ 5 = 2.4

∴ On average, each score is 2.4 points away from the mean of 6

✏️ Practice: Find the MAD

−5    1    3    7    9

Find the mean, then calculate the MAD. What does your MAD value tell you?

💡 The |absolute value| means ignore the negative sign when calculating deviations

Wrap-Up
⭐ Lesson Summary
📊

Mean

Sum of all values ÷ count. Works with decimals, fractions & negatives. Affected by extreme values.

📍

Median

Middle value when data is sorted. For even count: average the two middle values. Not affected by outliers.

🏆

Mode

Most frequent value. Data can have no mode, one mode, or be bimodal (two modes).

📏

Range

Max − Min. Measures spread. Subtracting negatives: a − (−b) = a + b. Large range = high variability.

📍

Line Plots

Dots on a number line showing data frequency. Read mode (tallest), range, median, and distribution.

🌿

Stem & Leaf

Organises data preserving original values. Stem = leading digits, Leaf = last digit. Always include a KEY.
