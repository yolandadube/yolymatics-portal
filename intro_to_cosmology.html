What is Cosmology?

Cosmology is the scientific study of the universe as a whole — its birth, evolution, structure, and eventual fate. It asks the biggest questions possible: How did the universe begin? How old is it? Where is it going?

Unlike astronomy, which studies individual objects like stars or galaxies, cosmology zooms out to the biggest possible picture.

Estimated age of the universe: approximately 13.8 billion years, based on measurements of the cosmic microwave background radiation and the observed expansion of the universe.

Many ideas in cosmology are models — our best attempts to explain the evidence. They are supported by strong observations, but science always remains open to revision as new data is gathered.

Why study cosmology?

🔭

Our Origins

Every atom in your body was forged inside a star. Understanding the universe means understanding ourselves.

🌌

Unified Physics

Cosmology brings together gravity, quantum mechanics, thermodynamics, and nuclear physics into one grand story.

❓

Open Questions

Dark matter, dark energy, and the multiverse are frontiers where discoveries are still being made today.

Panel 3 of 8  ·  Driving Question 1
The Dark Night Sky — Olbers' Paradox

Why is the night sky dark? This simple question turns out to be one of the most profound puzzles in cosmology — and its answer tells us something deep about the nature of the universe.

The Paradox

In the early 1800s, astronomer Heinrich Wilhelm Olbers pointed out a problem. If the universe is infinitely large, filled with infinite stars, and infinitely old — then every line of sight in every direction should eventually hit the surface of a star. The entire night sky should blaze as bright as the Sun!

Olbers' Paradox: If the universe is infinite, eternal, and uniformly filled with stars — the night sky should be blindingly bright. But it is dark. Why?

The Solution — What the Dark Sky Reveals

The Universe had a beginning

Light from stars more than ~13.8 billion light-years away has not had time to reach us. We can only see a finite region — the observable universe.

The Universe is expanding

As the universe expands, light from distant galaxies is stretched to longer wavelengths, eventually shifting out of visible light entirely — the "redshift" effect.

Stars are not eternal

Stars are born and die. They have not been shining forever, so not every point in the sky has a permanent star behind it.

Key Insight: The darkness of the night sky is one of the earliest clues that the universe has a finite age and is expanding — a simple observation with a profound conclusion!
Panel 4 of 8  ·  Driving Question 2
Evidence for the Big Bang

The Big Bang theory describes how the universe began as an incredibly hot, dense point and has been expanding and cooling ever since. It is not an explosion in space — it is space itself expanding.

📡

1. Expanding Universe

In 1929, Edwin Hubble showed that galaxies recede faster the farther they are — Hubble's Law. Running this backwards points to a single origin: the Big Bang.

🌡️

2. Cosmic Microwave Background

In 1965, Penzias and Wilson discovered faint microwave radiation filling the sky uniformly. The CMB is the cooled afterglow of the early hot universe — now just 2.7 K.

⚛️

3. H/He Abundance

Big Bang theory predicts that the early universe fused hydrogen into helium. The observed ~75% H / ~25% He in the oldest stars matches the predictions perfectly.

🌀

4. Large-Scale Structure

Tiny quantum fluctuations in the early universe grew into today's galaxies and clusters. CMB maps match computer simulations of structure formation beautifully.

The Cosmic Horizon

Because the universe has a finite age and light travels at a finite speed, there is a limit to how far we can see — the cosmic horizon. Light from beyond ~46 billion light-years has not reached us yet. This is larger than 13.8 billion light-years because space itself stretched while light was travelling to us.

Panel 5 of 8  ·  A Closer Look
The Expanding Universe

Hubble's discovery that galaxies are receding from us in every direction — and faster the farther away they are — is the cornerstone of modern cosmology.

Redshift — The Doppler Effect for Light

You have probably heard how the pitch of an ambulance siren drops as it passes you — the Doppler effect. The same thing happens with light: when a source moves away, its light waves are stretched to longer (redder) wavelengths. This is called redshift.

Analogy: Imagine dots drawn on a balloon. As you inflate it, every dot moves away from every other dot. No dot is at the "centre" — the whole surface is expanding. The universe works the same way, in three dimensions.

Hubble's Law

v = H₀ × d

Where v is recession speed, d is distance, and H₀ is the Hubble constant (~70 km/s per megaparsec). A galaxy 1 Mpc away moves at ~70 km/s; one at 2 Mpc moves at ~140 km/s.

Dark Energy and Accelerating Expansion

In 1998, astronomers discovered that the expansion is speeding up, not slowing down. This is driven by a mysterious force called dark energy.

Composition of the Universe today:
~5% ordinary matter (atoms)  ·  ~27% dark matter  ·  ~68% dark energy
Panel 6 of 8  ·  The Early Universe
First Stars & Matter History

The universe began with only hydrogen and helium. Through billions of years and countless stellar explosions, all the heavier elements — including those in your body — were created.

Cosmic Timeline

t = 0 — The Big Bang

An unimaginably hot, dense state. All known physics breaks down at this moment.

t ≈ 3 minutes — Nucleosynthesis

Protons and neutrons fuse into helium nuclei, producing the ~75% H / ~25% He ratio we observe in ancient stars.

t ≈ 380,000 years — Recombination

Electrons bind to nuclei forming neutral atoms. The universe becomes transparent and the CMB is released.

t ≈ 100–500 million years — First Stars (Pop. III)

Massive, short-lived stars made only of H and He ignite. Their supernova explosions seed space with carbon, oxygen, and iron for the first time.

t ≈ 9.2 billion years — Our Solar System

The Sun and planets condense from gas and dust enriched by previous stellar generations.

t = 13.8 billion years — Today

You are here — made of stardust, reading about your own cosmic history.

You are made of stardust — literally. Every element heavier than hydrogen in your body was forged inside a star that lived and died long before our Solar System existed.
Panel 7 of 8  ·  Driving Question 3
The Shape of the Universe

When cosmologists talk about the "shape" of the universe, they mean its large-scale geometry — how space itself is curved. This is determined by the total amount of mass and energy the universe contains.

🏔️

Closed (Ω > 1)

Positively curved — like a sphere. Parallel lines converge. Triangle angles > 180°. Could eventually collapse in a "Big Crunch."

📄

Flat (Ω = 1)

No curvature — like a flat sheet. Parallel lines stay parallel. Triangle angles = exactly 180°. This is what our best measurements suggest.

🏇

Open (Ω < 1)

Negatively curved — like a saddle. Parallel lines diverge. Triangle angles < 180°. Would expand forever.

What Observations Tell Us

Measurements of the CMB by the Planck satellite reveal that the universe appears to be spatially flat — the density parameter Ω is within 0.4% of the critical value of 1.

Flat geometry implies: The universe could be infinite in extent, expand forever, and geometry on the largest scales follows the familiar Euclidean rules you learn in school.

The Ultimate Fate

Combined with accelerating expansion from dark energy, the best current model suggests the universe will expand forever, eventually fading into a cold, dark, near-empty state — the Big Freeze or Heat Death.
