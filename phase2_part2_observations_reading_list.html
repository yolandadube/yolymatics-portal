DESI DR1 — The First Hint
6 million extragalactic objects — the first evidence for dynamical dark energy from BAO
Released
April 2024
Objects
> 6 million galaxies + quasars
Redshift range
\(0.1 < z < 4.2\)
Tracer types
BGS, LRG, ELG, QSO, Ly\(\alpha\)
BAO precision
\(\sim 0.5\%\) (competitive with all prior surveys combined)
What DESI DR1 found
DESI DR1 measured the BAO scale — the sound horizon \(r_d \approx 147\ \mathrm{Mpc}\) imprinted on galaxy clustering — at seven effective redshifts from \(z_{\rm eff} = 0.30\) to \(z_{\rm eff} = 2.33\).

BAO alone: consistent with flat \(\Lambda\)CDM (\(\Omega_m = 0.295 \pm 0.015\)).

BAO + CMB (Planck) + SNIa: When combined with supernovae, the best-fit \(w_0 w_a\)CDM parameters are: \[w_0 = -0.55^{+0.39}_{-0.21},\quad w_a = -1.32^{+0.58}_{-0.82}\] (DESI + CMB + DESY5 SNIa). This rejects \(\Lambda\)CDM (\(w_0=-1, w_a=0\)) at \(\sim 2.5\sigma\) to \(3.9\sigma\) depending on the SNe dataset used.

The key result: \(w_a < 0\) at high significance — dark energy was more negative (phantom-like, \(w < -1\)) in the past and is becoming less negative (quintessence-like, \(w > -1\)) today. This implies a phantom crossing at \(z \sim 0.5\).
What the tracer samples are
BGS (Bright Galaxy Survey): \(z_{\rm eff} \approx 0.30\). Low-\(z\) galaxies. Measures \(D_V(z)/r_d\) (volume-averaged distance).
LRG (Luminous Red Galaxies): \(z_{\rm eff} \approx 0.51, 0.71\). Split into two redshift bins. Measures \(D_M(z)/r_d\) and \(D_H(z)/r_d\) separately.
ELG (Emission Line Galaxies): \(z_{\rm eff} \approx 1.32\). High-\(z\) star-forming galaxies. Key new DESI contribution — highest signal-to-noise at this redshift.
QSO (Quasars): \(z_{\rm eff} \approx 1.49\). In DR1, only \(D_V/r_d\) measured. In DR2, split into \(D_M\) and \(D_H\) separately (improved signal).
Ly\(\alpha\) forest: \(z_{\rm eff} \approx 2.33\). Absorption in \(z > 2\) quasar spectra traces the intergalactic medium. Highest-redshift BAO measurement.
Key paper: DESI Collaboration (2024), "DESI 2024 VI: Cosmological Constraints from BAO", arXiv:2404.03002 [astro-ph.CO]

DESI DR2 — The Evidence Strengthens
14 million objects, 3 years of data — \(\Lambda\)CDM rejected at up to \(4.2\sigma\)
Released
March 2025
Objects
> 14 million (2.3\(\times\) DR1)
BAO precision
\(\sim 0.24\%\) (2\(\times\) better than DR1)
Rejection of \(\Lambda\)CDM
\(2.8\sigma\) to \(4.2\sigma\) (depends on SNe)
Neutrino mass limit
\(\sum m_\nu < 0.064\ \mathrm{eV}\) (\(\Lambda\)CDM)
What DESI DR2 found — the headline results
DR2 doubles the data volume and halves the BAO uncertainties compared to DR1. The QSO sample now has sufficient signal-to-noise for separate \(D_M(z)\) and \(D_H(z)\) measurements (in DR1 only \(D_V\) was reported).

Key result 1 — \(\Lambda\)CDM tension: BAO alone are in mild \(2.3\sigma\) tension with CMB-preferred \(\Lambda\)CDM parameters. Adding a time-varying dark energy EoS via \(w_0 w_a\)CDM alleviates this tension.

Key result 2 — Dynamical dark energy: Combined with CMB + SNIa, \(\Lambda\)CDM is rejected at: \[\begin{aligned} &2.8\sigma \quad (\text{Pantheon+}) \\ &3.8\sigma \quad (\text{Union3}) \\ &4.2\sigma \quad (\text{DESY5}) \end{aligned}\] The preference for dynamical DE does not diminish relative to DR1 — it strengthens.

Key result 3 — Phantom crossing preferred: Non-parametric reconstructions (Gaussian Processes, flexknots) and parametric fits all indicate \(w(z)\) crosses \(-1\) at \(z \approx 0.5\). Quintessence alone (\(w \geq -1\)) is disfavoured relative to models that allow crossing.

Key result 4 — Neutrino masses: \(\sum m_\nu < 0.064\ \mathrm{eV}\) (\(\Lambda\)CDM, 95% CL). In \(w_0 w_a\)CDM: \(\sum m_\nu < 0.16\ \mathrm{eV}\). Dynamical DE relaxes the neutrino mass tension.
Implications and caveats
Phantom crossing requires beyond-quintessence physics: A single canonical scalar field cannot cross \(w = -1\). This points to quintom, k-essence, or multi-field models — or a dynamical dark matter component.
SNe dataset dependence: The tension level depends on which SNIa compilation is used. Pantheon+ gives the weakest signal (\(2.8\sigma\)); DESY5 gives the strongest (\(4.2\sigma\)). This is a known systematic concern.
CPL parametrisation may be biased: At high \(z\), CPL forces \(w \to w_0 + w_a\) which can be very negative, potentially overweighting phantom behaviour. Non-parametric methods confirm the trend but with wider errors.
DESI DR3 (expected 2026–2027) with 5 years of data will settle whether this is a \(5\sigma\) detection or a statistical fluctuation. Your research timing is excellent — results from Euclid Early Release are also expected soon.
Key papers: DESI Collaboration (2025) arXiv:2503.14738 (DR2 BAO + cosmology). Lodha et al. (2025) arXiv:2503.14743 (extended DE analysis). Dinda & Maartens (2025) (your supervisor's analysis of DESI results).

● Topic 3  —  CMB constraints on dark energy 4 / 10
CMB Constraints on Dark Energy
How Planck, ACT, and SPT constrain the dark energy equation of state
How the CMB constrains dark energy
The CMB alone has limited direct sensitivity to dark energy because the last scattering surface is at \(z \approx 1100\) — when DE was negligible. However, the CMB constrains DE indirectly through:

1. The angular scale of the sound horizon: \[\theta_* = \frac{r_s(z_*)}{d_A(z_*)} \approx 1.0411^\circ\] Planck measures \(\theta_*\) to \(0.03\%\) precision. Since \(d_A(z_*)\) depends on the entire expansion history \(H(z)\) from \(z_*\) to today, changing DE modifies \(d_A\) and therefore changes \(\theta_*\) — unless other parameters are adjusted to compensate. This is the geometric degeneracy.

2. The ISW effect: At late times, when DE starts dominating, gravitational potentials decay. This causes a secondary temperature anisotropy \(\Delta T/T \propto \int \dot\Phi\, dt\) on large scales (\(\ell \lesssim 30\)). Stronger DE \(\to\) faster potential decay \(\to\) larger ISW signal. This is a direct, redshift-dependent probe of DE.

3. CMB lensing: The CMB photons are gravitationally lensed by intervening structure. The lensing power spectrum \(C_\ell^{\phi\phi}\) is sensitive to the growth of structure from \(z \sim 0.5\text{–}5\), which depends on DE through the growth factor \(D_+(z)\).
Planck 2018 constraints and beyond
Planck 2018 (CMB alone): \(w = -1.57^{+0.50}_{-0.40}\) in \(w\)CDM — very broad because the geometric degeneracy allows \(w\) to trade off against \(\Omega_m\) and \(H_0\). CMB alone cannot usefully constrain \(w_a\).
Planck + BAO: \(w_0 = -0.957 \pm 0.080\), \(w_a = -0.29^{+0.32}_{-0.26}\) — BAO breaks the degeneracy by providing distance measurements at multiple redshifts.
ACT DR6 (2023): The Atacama Cosmology Telescope provides an independent measurement of \(\theta_*\) and lensing, consistent with Planck. Combined: tighter \(\Omega_c h^2\) and \(n_s\) — helps constrain DE perturbations.
CMB-S4 (future, \(\sim 2030\)): Will measure \(\sigma(N_{\rm eff}) \approx 0.02\) and \(\sigma(\sum m_\nu) \approx 0.02\ \mathrm{eV}\) — tight enough to detect neutrino mass and new relativistic species. Combined with DESI DR5, will constrain \(w_0\) and \(w_a\) at percent level.
CMB + DESI DR2 finding: When combined, CMB constraints favour a phantom phase at early times (\(w < -1\) at \(z > 0.5\)), while late-universe data prefer quintessence (\(w > -1\) today). This phantom-quintessence tension is the central puzzle.
Key papers: Planck 2018 VI (arXiv:1807.06209). ACT DR6 (arXiv:2304.05203). Zhang, Xu & Chen (2026) arXiv:2512.07281 (multi-model DESI DR2 + CMB).

● Topic 4  —  Supernovae and weak lensing constraints 5 / 10
SNIa & Weak Lensing Constraints
Standard candles and cosmic shear — the late-universe probes of dark energy
Type Ia Supernovae — the three compilations
Pantheon+ (Brout et al. 2022): 1701 SNIa over \(0.001 < z < 2.26\). The most conservative dataset. Combined with DESI DR2 + CMB: rejects \(\Lambda\)CDM at \(2.8\sigma\).

Union3 (Rubin et al. 2023): Updated compilation with improved systematics. Combined with DESI DR2 + CMB: rejects \(\Lambda\)CDM at \(3.8\sigma\).

DESY5 (DES Collaboration 2024): 1635 SNIa from the Dark Energy Survey 5-year sample. The most constraining. Combined with DESI DR2 + CMB: rejects \(\Lambda\)CDM at \(4.2\sigma\).

How SNIa work: They are standardisable candles — after light-curve corrections (stretch, colour), their peak luminosity is nearly constant. They measure the luminosity distance: \[d_L(z) = (1+z)\int_0^z \frac{c\,dz'}{H(z')}\] The distance modulus \(\mu(z) = 5\log_{10}[d_L(z)/10\ \mathrm{pc}]\) is compared to theory to constrain \(w_0, w_a\).

Key systematic: The tension level between \(\Lambda\)CDM and DDE depends on which SNe sample is used. This suggests either unresolved SNe systematics or genuine new physics affecting the calibration differently.
Weak gravitational lensing
What WL measures: The distortion of background galaxy shapes by foreground mass — cosmic shear. The lensing power spectrum is proportional to \(\int W^2(z) P_\delta(k,z)\, dz\) and is directly sensitive to \(\sigma_8\) and \(\Omega_m\).
\(S_8\) parameter: \(S_8 = \sigma_8(\Omega_m/0.3)^{0.5}\). WL surveys (KiDS-1000, DES Y3, HSC) consistently find \(S_8 \approx 0.76\), while Planck CMB gives \(S_8 \approx 0.83\) — a \(2\text{–}3\sigma\) tension.
Why this matters for DDM: Dynamical dark matter with \(w_{\rm dm} \neq 0\) or decaying DDM suppresses \(P(k)\) at small scales, lowering \(\sigma_8\). This can resolve the \(S_8\) tension — a key motivation for DDM models.
KiDS-1000 + \(\Lambda\)DDM (Bucko et al. 2024): Decaying DM with velocity kick \(v_k \sim 100\ \mathrm{km/s}\) and lifetime \(1/\Gamma \sim 100\ \mathrm{Gyr}\) can reduce \(S_8\) tension from \(3\sigma\) to \(\lesssim 1\sigma\). This is the observational benchmark for DDM.
Euclid (launched July 2023): Will survey 15,000 deg\(^2\), measuring WL to unprecedented precision. Combined with spectroscopic galaxy clustering, will constrain \(w_0\) and \(w_a\) to \(\sim 1\%\) and test DDM models directly.
Key papers: Brout et al. (2022) arXiv:2202.04077 (Pantheon+). Asgari et al. (2021) arXiv:2007.15633 (KiDS-1000). Bucko et al. (2024) arXiv:2309.10038 (\(\Lambda\)DDM + WL).

● Topic 5  —  Alternative \(w(z)\) parametrisations beyond CPL 6 / 10
Beyond CPL — Alternative Parametrisations
Is the DESI signal real physics or a CPL artefact? These alternatives help answer that
Parametric alternatives to CPL
1. JBP (Jassal-Bagla-Padmanabhan): \[w(a) = w_0 + w_a\,a(1-a)\] Peaks at \(a = 0.5\) (\(z = 1\)) and vanishes at \(a \to 0\) and \(a = 1\). Better behaved at high \(z\) than CPL.

2. BA (Barboza-Alcaniz): \[w(z) = w_0 + w_a\frac{z(1+z)}{1+z^2}\] Bounded at all \(z\) — avoids the divergence problem. Peaks at \(z = 1\).

3. Logarithmic: \[w(a) = w_0 + w_a\ln a\] Simple and well-behaved. Commonly used in string-theory-motivated models.

4. Sigmoid / step models: \[w(z) = w_f + \frac{w_i - w_f}{1 + e^{(z-z_t)/\Delta z}}\] Allows a sharp transition at \(z_t\) with width \(\Delta z\). Tests whether the DESI signal is a smooth evolution or a sudden change.

5. Non-parametric (model-independent): Gaussian Processes (GP), flexknots, binned \(w(z)\) — let the data determine \(w(z)\) without assuming a functional form. DESI DR2 extended analysis (arXiv:2503.14743) uses all of these.
What the DESI extended analysis found
Consistency: All parametric and non-parametric methods find the same qualitative trend — \(w(z)\) evolves with redshift, with possible phantom crossing near \(z \approx 0.5\).
CPL is sufficient: Even with the extra flexibility of GP and binned \(w(z)\), extending \(\Lambda\)CDM with a two-parameter \(w(z)\) captures all trends present in the data. Higher-order terms are not needed.
Phantom crossing is preferred: Models with \(w \geq -1\) everywhere (pure quintessence) are disfavoured relative to models that allow crossing. This is consistent across all methods.
Dinda & Maartens (2025): Your supervisor's paper develops a model-agnostic diagnostic to test whether the DESI signal is robust. This is directly relevant to your research direction — ask Prof. Maartens about this on 17 May.
Key papers: Lodha et al. (2025) arXiv:2503.14743 (DESI DR2 extended DE). Dinda & Maartens (2025) arXiv:2406.11418 (model-agnostic DESI assessment). Roy (2024) arXiv:2406.12106 (alternative parametrisations).

● Topic 6  —  Growth rate \(f\sigma_8(z)\) and redshift-space distortions 7 / 10
Growth Rate & Redshift-Space Distortions
How galaxy surveys measure \(f\sigma_8(z)\) to test dark energy and modified gravity
Growth rate
\(\displaystyle f(z) \equiv \frac{d\ln D_+}{d\ln a} \approx \Omega_m(z)^{\gamma},\quad \gamma \approx 0.55\ (\Lambda\mathrm{CDM})\)
The physics of \(f\sigma_8\)
The growth rate \(f(z)\) measures how fast matter perturbations grow. In the linear regime: \[\delta''(\ln a) + \left(2 + \frac{H'}{H}\right)\delta'(\ln a) - \frac{3}{2}\Omega_m(a)\,\delta = 0\] The growing solution is \(\delta \propto D_+(a)\), and \(f = d\ln D_+/d\ln a\). In \(\Lambda\)CDM, the growth index \(\gamma \approx 0.55\) is a robust prediction. In dynamical DE: \(\gamma\) changes. In modified gravity: \(\gamma\) can be very different (e.g. \(\gamma \approx 0.68\) in DGP gravity).

Redshift-space distortions (RSD): Peculiar velocities distort galaxy clustering along the line of sight. On large scales (Kaiser regime): \[P^s(k,\mu) = (b + f\mu^2)^2 P(k)\] where \(b\) is galaxy bias and \(\mu = \hat{k}\cdot\hat{n}\). The combination \(f\sigma_8(z)\) is measured from the anisotropy of the galaxy power spectrum. This is a direct probe of the growth history — independent of geometry.

In DDM models, \(f\sigma_8\) is modified because the effective matter density entering the growth equation changes: \(\rho_{\rm dm}^{\rm eff}\) depends on whether DM decays, has \(w_{\rm dm} \neq 0\), or interacts with DE.
Current measurements and DDM impact
BOSS DR12: \(f\sigma_8(z=0.32) = 0.427 \pm 0.056\) and \(f\sigma_8(z=0.57) = 0.426 \pm 0.029\) — consistent with \(\Lambda\)CDM predictions but with room for deviation.
DESI full-shape analysis: DESI DR2 includes the first DESI full-shape galaxy power spectrum analysis (beyond BAO), constraining \(f\sigma_8(z)\) at multiple redshifts. This tightens \(w_0, w_a\) constraints and tests modified gravity.
EFT of dark energy: Ishak et al. (2024) use the DESI full-shape data to constrain the EFT of DE parameters \(\Omega_0\) and \(\beta\) — no significant deviation from GR found yet, but constraints will tighten with DR3.
Why this matters for you: Your bispectrum work includes RSD and wide-angle effects. In DDM, the Kaiser formula is modified: \(P^s(k,\mu) = (b + f_{\rm DDM}\mu^2)^2 P_{\rm DDM}(k)\) where \(f_{\rm DDM}\) and \(P_{\rm DDM}(k)\) are both changed. The bispectrum adds \(F_2\) kernel corrections.
Key papers: DESI Collaboration (2025) arXiv:2503.14745 (full-shape). Ishak et al. (2024) (EFT of DE with DESI). Guzzo et al. (2008) Nature 451, 541 (first \(f\sigma_8\) measurement from RSD).

● Topic 7  —  Future surveys — Euclid, MegaMapper, Rubin, CMB-S4 8 / 10
Future Surveys — Where Your Research Fits
The next-generation instruments that will test dynamical dark energy and DDM
The key future surveys
Euclid (ESA, launched July 2023): 15,000 deg\(^2\) imaging + spectroscopy. 30 million galaxy redshifts at \(0.9 < z < 1.8\). Will measure \(w_0\) to \(\pm 0.025\) and \(w_a\) to \(\pm 0.1\). Weak lensing + galaxy clustering + galaxy-galaxy lensing. Early Release data expected 2025–2026.
MegaMapper (proposed, \(\sim 2030\)s): \(z = 2\text{–}5\) Lyman-break galaxy (LBG) survey. 100 million+ spectra. This is the survey from your JCAP paper — you are working on its science case. Unique high-\(z\) reach gives direct access to the turnover scale \(k_0\) at multiple redshifts.
Vera Rubin LSST (\(\sim 2025\) start): 10-year photometric survey. 20 billion galaxies. Will discover \(\sim 10^5\) SNIa per year. Photometric BAO, WL, galaxy clusters. Enormous statistical power for \(w(z)\) reconstruction.
CMB-S4 (\(\sim 2030\)): Ground-based CMB. \(\sigma(r) < 0.001\), \(\sigma(N_{\rm eff}) < 0.02\), \(\sigma(\sum m_\nu) < 0.02\ \mathrm{eV}\). Combined with DESI/Euclid: definitive test of DDM and dynamical DE.
SKA (Square Kilometre Array, first light \(\sim 2027\)): 21-cm intensity mapping for BAO at \(z > 1\). Radio continuum survey for WL. HI galaxy survey. Complementary to optical/IR surveys — probes different systematic errors.
How your research connects
Your published JCAP paper (arXiv:2511.06962) on turnover detection with \(P(k)\) and \(B(k_1,k_2,k_3)\) is a direct forecast for MegaMapper LBGs. Extending this to DDM means: computing how DDM changes the turnover \(k_0\), the growth rate \(f\sigma_8\), and the bispectrum kernel \(F_2\).
Euclid forecasts for DDM: No published Fisher forecast exists for DDM parameters (\(w_{\rm dm,0}\), \(w_{\rm dm,a}\), or \(\Gamma\), \(v_k\)) using Euclid galaxy clustering. This is an open gap your research can fill.
Relativistic effects in DDM: Your expertise in the relativistic galaxy bispectrum (Doppler, lensing, wide-angle terms) is directly extendable to DDM models — where these corrections have not been computed.
Dinda & Maartens work: Your supervisor Bikash R. Dinda (your co-author) and Roy Maartens have published on model-agnostic DESI diagnostics. This is the group whose methodology you will use and extend.
Your competitive advantage: You sit at the intersection of relativistic perturbation theory + bispectrum expertise + Euclid/MegaMapper forecasting. This combination is rare and positions you to make unique contributions to DDM research.
