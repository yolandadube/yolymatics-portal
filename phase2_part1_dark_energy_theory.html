● Topic 1  —  The cosmological constant \(\Lambda\) 2 / 10
The Cosmological Constant \(\Lambda\)
The simplest dark energy model — a constant energy density filling all of spacetime
Einstein field eqs
\(\displaystyle G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G\, T_{\mu\nu}\)
What \(\Lambda\) is — from Einstein's equations
Einstein introduced \(\Lambda\) in 1917 to obtain a static universe. When expansion was discovered (Hubble, 1929), he called it his "greatest blunder." It was revived in 1998 when Type Ia supernovae showed the expansion is accelerating (Riess et al. 1998; Perlmutter et al. 1999 — Nobel Prize 2011).

Moving \(\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}\) to the right-hand side of Einstein's equation, it acts as a perfect fluid with constant energy density and negative pressure: \[\rho_\Lambda = \frac{\Lambda c^2}{8\pi G}, \qquad p_\Lambda = -\rho_\Lambda c^2\] The equation of state is therefore exactly \(w = p/\rho c^2 = -1\) — constant in time and space. This negative pressure is what drives the accelerated expansion: from the second Friedmann equation, \[\frac{\ddot{a}}{a} = -\frac{4\pi G}{3}\left(\rho + \frac{3p}{c^2}\right) + \frac{\Lambda}{3}\] acceleration (\(\ddot{a} > 0\)) occurs when \(\Lambda/3 > (4\pi G/3)(\rho + 3p/c^2)\), which is satisfied when \(\Lambda\) dominates over matter.
Key equations and properties
Friedmann equation with \(\Lambda\):
\(H^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho_m - \frac{k}{a^2} + \frac{\Lambda}{3}\)
Energy density parameters:
\(\Omega_m + \Omega_\Lambda + \Omega_k = 1\) where \(\Omega_\Lambda = \Lambda/(3H_0^2)\)
Observed value (Planck 2018):
\(\Omega_\Lambda \approx 0.685\), so \(\rho_\Lambda \approx 5.96 \times 10^{-27}\ \mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}\)
\(\Lambda\) does not dilute:
\(\rho_\Lambda = \mathrm{const}\), while \(\rho_m \propto a^{-3}\) and \(\rho_r \propto a^{-4}\). This is why \(\Lambda\) dominates at late times.
de Sitter limit: When \(\Lambda\) fully dominates: \(a(t) \propto e^{Ht}\) with \(H = \sqrt{\Lambda/3}\) — exponential expansion forever

● Topic 2  —  The cosmological constant problem 3 / 10
The Cosmological Constant Problem
The worst fine-tuning problem in all of physics — 120 orders of magnitude
The old cosmological constant problem
In quantum field theory, every field has a zero-point energy — vacuum fluctuations that contribute to the energy density of empty space. For a scalar field of mass \(m\), each mode \(k\) contributes \(\frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega_k\). Summing over all modes up to a cutoff \(\Lambda_{\rm UV}\): \[\rho_{\rm vac}^{\rm QFT} \sim \frac{\Lambda_{\rm UV}^4}{16\pi^2 \hbar^3 c^3}\] If we take the cutoff at the Planck scale \(\Lambda_{\rm UV} \sim m_{\mathrm{P}} c\): \[\rho_{\rm vac}^{\rm QFT} \sim m_{\mathrm{P}}^4 c^3 / \hbar^3 \sim 10^{96}\ \mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}\] But the observed vacuum energy density from cosmology is: \[\rho_\Lambda^{\rm obs} \approx 5.96 \times 10^{-27}\ \mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}\] The discrepancy is \(\sim 10^{123}\) orders of magnitude — the largest disagreement between theory and experiment in the history of physics (Weinberg 1989, Rev. Mod. Phys. 61, 1).

Even using the electroweak scale \(\Lambda_{\rm UV} \sim 100\ \mathrm{GeV}\), the prediction is \(\sim 10^{55}\) times too large.
The new cosmological constant problem & coincidence problem
The new problem (Weinberg 2000): Even if we accept \(\rho_\Lambda\) is small, why is it of the same order as \(\rho_m\) today? Since \(\rho_m \propto a^{-3}\) but \(\rho_\Lambda = \mathrm{const}\), they are equal only at one epoch — and we happen to live in it.
Coincidence problem: \(\Omega_\Lambda/\Omega_m \approx 2.2\) today. For most of cosmic history this ratio was either \(\sim 0\) (matter dominated) or \(\to \infty\) (far future). Why now?
Five approaches (Weinberg 1989): (1) Symmetry principle forcing \(\Lambda = 0\), (2) Supersymmetry (boson-fermion cancellation), (3) Quintessence (dynamical DE), (4) Anthropic principle, (5) Quantum cosmology
SUSY does not solve it: In exact SUSY, \(\rho_{\rm vac} = 0\) (bosons cancel fermions). But SUSY is broken at \(\Lambda_{\rm SUSY} \gtrsim 1\ \mathrm{TeV}\), so \(\rho_{\rm vac} \sim \Lambda_{\rm SUSY}^4 \sim 10^{60}\times\rho_\Lambda^{\rm obs}\)
This problem is the primary motivation for dynamical dark energy — if DE is not \(\Lambda\) but a field that evolves, the coincidence problem may find a dynamical explanation
Key reference: Weinberg (1989), "The Cosmological Constant Problem", Rev. Mod. Phys. 61, 1 — the foundational paper. Also: Carroll (2001), "The Cosmological Constant", Living Rev. Relativity 4, 1.

● Topic 3  —  Quintessence — the action and field equations 4 / 10
Quintessence — From the Action
A canonical scalar field \(\phi\) with potential \(V(\phi)\) — the simplest dynamical dark energy model
Action
\(\displaystyle S = \int d^4x\,\sqrt{-g}\left[\frac{R}{16\pi G} - \frac{1}{2}g^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial_\nu\phi - V(\phi)\right] + S_m\)
Derivation of the field equations
The quintessence field \(\phi\) is a real scalar field minimally coupled to gravity with a canonical kinetic term. Varying the action with respect to \(\phi\) gives the Klein-Gordon equation in an expanding FLRW background: \[\ddot\phi + 3H\dot\phi + V'(\phi) = 0\] where \(V'(\phi) = dV/d\phi\) and \(H = \dot{a}/a\). The term \(3H\dot\phi\) is Hubble friction — the expansion of the universe slows the field's evolution.

The energy-momentum tensor of the scalar field takes the form of a perfect fluid: \[\rho_\phi = \frac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2 + V(\phi), \qquad p_\phi = \frac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2 - V(\phi)\] The equation of state is therefore: \[w_\phi = \frac{p_\phi}{\rho_\phi} = \frac{\dot\phi^2/2 - V(\phi)}{\dot\phi^2/2 + V(\phi)}\] When the field rolls slowly (\(\dot\phi^2 \ll V\)): \(w_\phi \to -1\) — mimics a cosmological constant. When the kinetic energy dominates (\(\dot\phi^2 \gg V\)): \(w_\phi \to +1\) — stiff matter.

For any canonical scalar field: \(-1 \leq w_\phi \leq +1\). Quintessence cannot cross the phantom divide \(w = -1\) — this is a fundamental theoretical constraint.
Key equations — derive these from scratch
Klein-Gordon: \(\ddot\phi + 3H\dot\phi + V'(\phi) = 0\) — equation of motion for \(\phi\) in FLRW
Friedmann with \(\phi\): \(H^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\bigl[\rho_m + \frac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2 + V(\phi)\bigr]\) — scalar field enters on the right
Continuity: \(\dot\rho_\phi + 3H(1 + w_\phi)\rho_\phi = 0\) — follows from \(\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0\)
Slow-roll conditions: \(\dot\phi^2 \ll V(\phi)\) and \(\ddot\phi \ll 3H\dot\phi\) — then \(w_\phi \approx -1\) and \(3H\dot\phi \approx -V'(\phi)\)
Slow-roll parameter: \(\epsilon_V = \frac{m_{\mathrm{P}}^2}{16\pi}\left(\frac{V'}{V}\right)^2 \ll 1\) — ensures \(w_\phi \approx -1 + \frac{2}{3}\epsilon_V\)
Fundamental bound: \(-1 \leq w_\phi \leq +1\) for canonical scalar fields — no phantom crossing is possible

● Topic 4  —  Quintessence potentials and their dynamics 5 / 10
Quintessence Potentials
The potential \(V(\phi)\) determines all the dynamics — here are the most studied forms
The major quintessence potentials
1. Exponential potential (Ratra & Peebles 1988): \[V(\phi) = V_0\, e^{-\lambda\phi/m_{\mathrm{P}}}\] Has an exact scaling solution where \(\Omega_\phi\) tracks \(\Omega_m\) during matter domination with \(w_\phi = w_m\) if \(\lambda^2 > 3(1 + w_m)\). This naturally addresses the coincidence problem. Arises in string theory compactifications.

2. Inverse power-law (Ratra & Peebles 1988): \[V(\phi) = M^{4+\alpha}\phi^{-\alpha}, \quad \alpha > 0\] A tracker potential — solutions from very different initial conditions converge to a common evolutionary track. This removes the fine-tuning of initial conditions. The field freezes when \(V(\phi)\) flattens sufficiently.

3. SUGRA potential (Brax & Martin 1999): \[V(\phi) = M^{4+\alpha}\phi^{-\alpha}\,e^{4\pi\phi^2/m_{\mathrm{P}}^2}\] The exponential correction from supergravity causes the field to freeze earlier, bringing \(w_\phi\) closer to \(-1\) today.

4. Hilltop / thawing potentials: \[V(\phi) = V_0\bigl[1 + (\phi/\mu)^n\bigr]^{-1}\quad \text{or}\quad V(\phi) = V_0\bigl[1 - (\phi/f)^2 + \ldots\bigr]\] The field is initially frozen at \(w \approx -1\) on a flat hilltop and only recently begins to roll (thaw).
Tracking vs thawing — the two classes
Tracking (freezing) models: The field tracks the dominant energy component (\(\rho_r\) or \(\rho_m\)) during early epochs, then freezes at late times as \(V(\phi)\) flattens. The EoS decreases toward \(-1\). Examples: inverse power-law, exponential, SUGRA.
Thawing models: The field is frozen at \(w \approx -1\) early on (Hubble friction dominates). As \(H\) decreases, the field starts to roll and \(w\) increases away from \(-1\). Examples: hilltop, pseudo-Nambu-Goldstone boson (axion-like).
Caldwell & Linder (2005) showed these two classes occupy distinct regions in \((w_0, w_a)\) space. Thawing: \(w_a \approx -3(1 + w_0)\). Freezing: \(w_a \approx 3w_0(1 + w_0)\).
Observational status (Planck 2018): Tracking freezing models are nearly indistinguishable from \(\Lambda\)CDM. Thawing models are constrained to \(w_0 < -0.7\) at 95% CL (Tsujikawa 2013 review).
DESI 2024/2025 hint: The best-fit \(w_0 \approx -0.73\), \(w_a \approx -1.05\) — this is in the thawing region with phantom crossing, which is not possible for canonical quintessence. This motivates phantom or multi-field models.
Key references: Ratra & Peebles (1988) PRD 37, 3406. Caldwell & Linder (2005) PRL 95, 141301. Tsujikawa (2013) CQG 30, 214003 [arXiv:1304.1961].

● Topic 5  —  k-essence — non-canonical kinetic terms 6 / 10
k-Essence
Generalising the kinetic term — dark energy from non-canonical scalar field dynamics
Action
\(\displaystyle S = \int d^4x\,\sqrt{-g}\left[\frac{R}{16\pi G} + \mathcal{L}(\phi, X)\right] + S_m, \qquad X \equiv -\tfrac{1}{2}g^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial_\nu\phi\)
The k-essence framework
k-Essence generalises quintessence by allowing a non-canonical kinetic term. The Lagrangian is a general function \(\mathcal{L}(\phi, X)\) of the field \(\phi\) and its kinetic energy \(X = -\frac{1}{2}(\partial\phi)^2\). Quintessence is the special case \(\mathcal{L} = X - V(\phi)\).

The energy density and pressure are: \[\rho = 2X\mathcal{L}_X - \mathcal{L}, \qquad p = \mathcal{L}\] where \(\mathcal{L}_X \equiv \partial\mathcal{L}/\partial X\). The equation of state is: \[w = \frac{\mathcal{L}}{2X\mathcal{L}_X - \mathcal{L}}\] The sound speed of perturbations is: \[c_s^2 = \frac{\mathcal{L}_X}{\mathcal{L}_X + 2X\mathcal{L}_{XX}}\] For quintessence: \(c_s^2 = 1\). For general k-essence: \(c_s^2\) can be different from 1, which modifies the growth of perturbations and the matter power spectrum.

Stability requires: \(\mathcal{L}_X > 0\) (no ghosts) and \(c_s^2 > 0\) (no gradient instabilities). These are non-trivial constraints that restrict the allowed Lagrangians.
Key facts and examples
DBI (Dirac-Born-Infeld) k-essence:
\(\mathcal{L} = -f(\phi)^{-1}\sqrt{1 - 2f(\phi)X} + f(\phi)^{-1} - V(\phi)\)
Arises from D-brane dynamics in string theory. Sound speed: \(c_s^2 = 1 - 2fX\).
Purely kinetic k-essence: \(\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}(X)\) only (no potential). Can produce tracking behaviour and late-time acceleration purely from kinetic energy dynamics.
Why it matters for your research: k-essence modifies the sound speed \(c_s\) of DE perturbations. This changes the ISW effect, the matter power spectrum \(P(k)\) at large scales, and the bispectrum — all directly relevant to Euclid and MegaMapper.
General framework: k-essence is a subset of Horndeski gravity — the most general scalar-tensor theory with second-order field equations (Horndeski 1974). The EFT of dark energy (Gubitosi, Piazza & Vernizzi 2013) parametrises all such theories.
Key references: Armendariz-Picon et al. (2001) PRD 63, 103510 [arXiv:astro-ph/0006373]. Copeland, Sami & Tsujikawa (2006) IJMPD 15, 1753 [arXiv:hep-th/0603057] — comprehensive DE review.

● Topic 6  —  Phantom dark energy (\(w < -1\)) 7 / 10
Phantom Dark Energy
When the equation of state crosses the phantom divide \(w = -1\) — and why DESI hints at this
Phantom action
\(\displaystyle S_{\rm phantom} = \int d^4x\,\sqrt{-g}\left[\frac{R}{16\pi G} \,\mathbf{+}\, \frac{1}{2}g^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial_\nu\phi - V(\phi)\right]\)   (note the + sign on the kinetic term)
What phantom dark energy is
A phantom field has a wrong-sign kinetic term: the kinetic energy is \(-\frac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2\) instead of \(+\frac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2\). This gives: \[\rho_\phi = -\frac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2 + V(\phi), \qquad p_\phi = -\frac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2 - V(\phi)\] \[w_\phi = \frac{-\dot\phi^2/2 - V}{-\dot\phi^2/2 + V} = \frac{\dot\phi^2/2 + V}{\dot\phi^2/2 - V}\] When \(V > \dot\phi^2/2\): \(w_\phi < -1\) — the phantom regime. The dark energy density grows with time: \(\rho_\phi \propto a^{-3(1+w)}\) with \(1 + w < 0\) means \(\rho_\phi\) increases as \(a\) grows.

This leads to the Big Rip: the scale factor diverges in finite time: \[t_{\rm rip} - t_0 = \frac{2}{3|1+w|H_0\sqrt{1-\Omega_m}}\] At \(t_{\rm rip}\), the Hubble rate \(H \to \infty\), and gravitationally bound systems are torn apart.

The fundamental problem: a wrong-sign kinetic term means the theory has a ghost instability — the vacuum is unstable. Quantum corrections drive the field to infinite energy in finite time. This makes single-field phantom models theoretically pathological.
Phantom crossing and the quintom scenario
No-go theorem: A single canonical minimally coupled scalar field cannot cross \(w = -1\). Proof: at the crossing, \(\dot\phi = 0\) and the EoS is undefined (Vikman 2005).
Quintom scenario (Feng et al. 2005): Use two fields — one quintessence (\(w > -1\)) and one phantom (\(w < -1\)). The combined EoS can smoothly cross \(w = -1\). \(\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}(\partial\phi_1)^2 - \frac{1}{2}(\partial\phi_2)^2 - V(\phi_1, \phi_2)\).
k-essence crossing: Non-canonical kinetic terms can achieve crossing without ghosts if \(c_s^2\) remains positive. This requires careful Lagrangian design.
Why DESI makes this critical: DESI DR1/DR2 best-fit CPL values \(w_0 \approx -0.73\), \(w_a \approx -1.05\) imply \(w(z)\) was phantom at \(z \gtrsim 0.5\) and is quintessence today. This requires phantom crossing — ruling out single-field quintessence.
EFT approach: The EFT of dark energy (Bellini & Sawicki 2014) parametrises all stable theories that can cross \(w = -1\) without invoking explicit ghost fields — currently the preferred theoretical framework.
Key references: Caldwell (2002) PLB 545, 23 (phantom DE). Feng et al. (2005) PLB 607, 35 (quintom). Vikman (2005) PRD 71, 023515 (no-go theorem).

● Topic 7  —  The Chevallier-Polarski-Linder (CPL) parametrisation 8 / 10
The CPL Parametrisation
The standard two-parameter model for the dark energy equation of state — used by DESI, Euclid, and all surveys
CPL equation of state
\(\displaystyle w(a) = w_0 + w_a(1-a) = w_0 + w_a\frac{z}{1+z}\)
Complete derivation of the background equations
Step 1 — EoS definition: \(w(a) = w_0 + w_a(1-a)\), where \(w_0 = w(a=1)\) is the present value and \(w_a = -dw/da|_{a=1}\) is the derivative at the present epoch. Proposed by Chevallier & Polarski (2001, IJMPD 10, 213) and Linder (2003, PRL 90, 091301).

Step 2 — Dark energy density: From the continuity equation \(\dot\rho_{\rm de} + 3H(1+w)\rho_{\rm de} = 0\), changing variable to \(a\): \[\frac{d\rho_{\rm de}}{\rho_{\rm de}} = -3\frac{1+w(a)}{a}\,da\] Integrating from \(a\) to 1 with \(w(a') = w_0 + w_a(1-a')\): \[\rho_{\rm de}(a) = \rho_{\rm de,0}\, a^{-3(1+w_0+w_a)}\, e^{-3w_a(1-a)}\] This is exact. Note the mixed power-law and exponential dependence.

Step 3 — Friedmann equation: For a flat universe: \[H^2(a) = H_0^2\left[\Omega_m\,a^{-3} + (1-\Omega_m)\,a^{-3(1+w_0+w_a)}\,e^{-3w_a(1-a)}\right]\] \(\Lambda\)CDM is recovered at \(w_0 = -1, w_a = 0\). \(w\)CDM is \(w_a = 0\).

Step 4 — Limits: At \(a \to 0\) (early times): \(w \to w_0 + w_a\). At \(a = 1\) (today): \(w = w_0\). The EoS evolves monotonically from \(w_0 + w_a\) to \(w_0\).
Properties and interpretation
\(w_0\) meaning: The dark energy equation of state today. \(w_0 = -1\) is \(\Lambda\)CDM. \(w_0 > -1\) is quintessence-like. \(w_0 < -1\) is phantom-like.
\(w_a\) meaning: The rate of change of \(w\) with scale factor at the present. \(w_a > 0\) means \(w\) was more negative in the past (phantom → quintessence). \(w_a < 0\) means \(w\) was less negative in the past (quintessence → phantom).
Phantom crossing: Occurs when \(w = -1\) at some \(a_*\), i.e. \(a_* = 1 + (1+w_0)/w_a\). The DESI best-fit implies crossing at \(z \approx 0.5\).
Limitation at high \(z\): As \(a \to 0\), \(w \to w_0 + w_a\) which can be very negative. This causes unphysical dark energy domination at early times. The CPL form is reliable only for \(z \lesssim 2\text{–}3\).
Mapping to quintessence: Thawing models cluster near \(w_a \approx -3(1+w_0)\). Freezing models near \(w_a \approx 3w_0(1+w_0)\) (Caldwell & Linder 2005).
Key references: Chevallier & Polarski (2001) IJMPD 10, 213 [gr-qc/0009008]. Linder (2003) PRL 90, 091301 [astro-ph/0208512]. Scherrer (2015) PRD 92, 043001 [arXiv:1505.05781].

● Topic 8  —  The \(w_0 w_a\)CDM model — full cosmological framework 9 / 10
The \(w_0 w_a\)CDM Model
The full 8-parameter cosmological model used by DESI, Euclid, and next-generation surveys
Parameters
8: \(\Omega_b h^2, \Omega_c h^2, \theta_*, \tau, n_s, A_s, w_0, w_a\)
\(\Lambda\)CDM limit
\(w_0 = -1,\ w_a = 0\)
\(w\)CDM limit
\(w_a = 0\)
The complete \(w_0 w_a\)CDM equations
Background expansion: \[H^2(z) = H_0^2\bigl[\Omega_r(1+z)^4 + \Omega_m(1+z)^3 + \Omega_{\rm de}(z)\bigr]\] with the dark energy density: \[\Omega_{\rm de}(z) = (1 - \Omega_m - \Omega_r)(1+z)^{3(1+w_0+w_a)}\exp\!\left[\frac{-3w_a z}{1+z}\right]\] Distances: \[d_C(z) = c\int_0^z \frac{dz'}{H(z')}, \quad d_A(z) = \frac{d_C(z)}{1+z}, \quad d_L(z) = (1+z)\,d_C(z)\] Growth of structure: In the linear regime, \[\delta''(\ln a) + \left(2 + \frac{H'}{H}\right)\delta'(\ln a) - \frac{3}{2}\Omega_m(a)\,\delta = 0\] where \(\Omega_m(a) = \Omega_{m,0}\,a^{-3}/E^2(a)\) and \(E(a) = H(a)/H_0\). The growth rate \(f = d\ln\delta/d\ln a\) is modified by the changing DE density.

Key observables that constrain \(w_0, w_a\): BAO measures \(d_A(z)/r_s\) and \(H(z)r_s\) at multiple redshifts. SNIa measure \(d_L(z)\). CMB measures \(d_A(z_*)\) and \(\theta_* = r_s/d_A(z_*)\). RSD measures \(f\sigma_8(z)\). Weak lensing measures \(\sigma_8(\Omega_m/0.3)^{0.5}\).
How each probe constrains the model
BAO (DESI, BOSS, eBOSS): Measures \(D_M(z)/r_d\) and \(D_H(z)/r_d\) at \(z = 0.3\text{–}4.2\). Constrains \(H(z)\) hence \(w_0, w_a\). DESI DR2 rejects \(\Lambda\)CDM at \(2.8\text{–}4.2\sigma\) depending on SNe dataset.
Type Ia Supernovae (Pantheon+, DESY5, Union3): Standard candles measuring \(d_L(z)\). Combined with BAO breaks the \(\Omega_m\)–\(w_0\) degeneracy. Different SNe compilations give different tension levels with \(\Lambda\)CDM.
CMB (Planck, ACT, SPT): Constrains \(\Omega_b h^2\), \(\Omega_c h^2\), \(\theta_*\), \(\tau\) precisely. Provides the early-universe anchor. Combined with BAO+SNIa determines \(w_0, w_a\).
RSD (\(f\sigma_8\)): Galaxy redshift surveys measure the growth rate \(f\sigma_8(z)\) from anisotropic clustering. In \(w_0 w_a\)CDM, growth is modified — \(f\sigma_8\) is a powerful discriminator between \(\Lambda\)CDM and DDE.
Power spectrum turnover \(k_0\): Your work — the turnover encodes \(\Omega_m h\), which changes when dark energy modifies \(H(z)\) at matter-radiation equality. DDM further modifies this via changed DM density evolution.

● Summary — All dark energy models at a glance 10 / 10
Summary — Dark Energy Theory
Every model from this Part 1, compared side by side — master these before moving to Part 2
The six dark energy frameworks — hierarchy
\(\Lambda\)CDM: \(w = -1\) exactly. Constant \(\rho_\Lambda\). 6 parameters. Fine-tuning: \(10^{123}\). Coincidence problem. Simplest model. Consistent with most data. DESI DR2 rejects at \(2.8\text{–}4.2\sigma\).
Quintessence: \(-1 < w_\phi \leq +1\). Canonical scalar \(\phi\) with \(V(\phi)\). Two classes: tracking (freezing) and thawing. Cannot cross \(w = -1\). Sound speed \(c_s = 1\).
k-Essence: General \(\mathcal{L}(\phi, X)\). Non-canonical kinetic term. Allows \(c_s \neq 1\). DBI from string theory. Modifies perturbation growth. Subset of Horndeski gravity.
Phantom: \(w < -1\). Wrong-sign kinetic term. Ghost instability. Energy grows. Big Rip in finite time. Single-field phantom is pathological.
Quintom: Two fields (\(\phi_1\) quintessence + \(\phi_2\) phantom). Can cross \(w = -1\) smoothly. Matches DESI best-fit. More parameters. Stability must be checked.
CPL / \(w_0 w_a\)CDM: Phenomenological. \(w(a) = w_0 + w_a(1-a)\). 8 parameters. Standard DESI/Euclid analysis framework. Not tied to a specific theory — it parametrises ignorance.
