Introduction
What is Electromagnetic Radiation?
⚡
Electric & Magnetic Fields
EM radiation consists of oscillating electric (E) and magnetic (B) fields that travel together and are always perpendicular to each other and to the direction of travel.
🌌
No Medium Needed
Unlike sound, EM waves do not need a medium — they travel through the vacuum of space at the speed of light: c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s.
c = f × λ   |   c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s
c = speed of light  ·  f = frequency (Hz)  ·  λ = wavelength (m)
Key Properties
Properties of EM Waves
〰️
Wavelength (λ)
Distance between two successive crests (or troughs). Measured in metres. Shorter λ = more energy.
🔄
Frequency (f)
Number of complete wave cycles per second. Measured in Hertz (Hz). Higher f = higher energy.
⚡
Energy (E)
E = hf where h = Planck's constant (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s). Higher frequency = higher energy photon.
🚀
Speed in Vacuum
All EM waves travel at exactly c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s in a vacuum — the fastest speed possible in the universe.
📐
Transverse Waves
EM waves are transverse — the oscillation is perpendicular to the direction of energy propagation.
↔️
Polarisation
EM waves can be polarised — the E-field oscillates in one specific plane. Used in sunglasses and LCD screens.
📌 Key inverse relationship: λ × f = c  →  As wavelength increases, frequency decreases, and the photon energy decreases.

The Spectrum — Part 1
📻 Radio Waves
Key Facts
• Longest wavelength (cm to km) and lowest frequency
• Lowest energy of all EM waves
• Produced by oscillating electric charges in antennas
• Can travel very long distances
• Some reflect off the ionosphere
λ : 0.1 m → 100 km  |  f : 3 Hz → 3 GHz
📺
TV Broadcasting
Terrestrial and satellite TV signals are transmitted as radio waves
🎙️
AM/FM Radio
AM uses longer waves that reflect off ionosphere; FM uses shorter line-of-sight waves
🧲
MRI Scans
Radio waves used with strong magnets to image soft tissue in the body — no ionising radiation
📶
Mobile Phones
Cell networks (3G/4G/5G) transmit data using radio wave frequencies
The Spectrum — Part 2
📡 Microwaves
Key Facts
• Wavelength: 1 mm to 10 cm
• Frequency: 300 MHz – 300 GHz
• Water molecules absorb microwaves very efficiently
• Can penetrate clouds, smoke and rain
• Used in telecommunications and cooking
λ : 1 mm → 10 cm  |  f : ~300 MHz → 300 GHz
🍕
Microwave Oven
2.45 GHz microwaves cause water molecules to vibrate rapidly, generating heat from within the food
✈️
RADAR
Radio Detection And Ranging — used in aviation, shipping, weather forecasting, and speed guns
📡
Satellite Communication
Microwaves pass easily through Earth's atmosphere — used for GPS, satellite TV, and internet
📶
WiFi & Bluetooth
Home and office wireless networks use microwave frequencies (2.4 GHz and 5 GHz)
The Spectrum — Part 3
🌡️ Infrared Radiation
Key Facts
• Wavelength: 700 nm – 1 mm
• Experienced as heat — all warm objects emit IR
• Just below the red end of visible light
• Cannot be seen by the human eye
• Detected by special cameras and sensors
λ : 700 nm → 1 mm
🌡️ Hotter objects emit IR at shorter wavelengths and much greater intensity (Stefan–Boltzmann law)
📷
Thermal Imaging
Infrared cameras detect heat signatures — used by fire services, military, and medical diagnosis
🎮
Remote Controls
TV remotes use IR pulses to send signals to devices — line-of-sight required
🔭
Astronomy
IR telescopes (like James Webb) reveal cool stars, dust clouds and distant galaxies
🏥
Physiotherapy
IR lamps used to warm muscles, relieve pain, and improve blood circulation in injured tissue
The Spectrum — Part 4
🌈 Visible Light
Key Facts
• Only part of the spectrum the human eye detects
• Wavelength: 400 nm (violet) – 700 nm (red)
• White light is a mixture of all colours
• Each colour has a different frequency and energy
• Can be refracted (prism, rainbow) and diffracted
RED 700nm ORANGE YELLOW GREEN BLUE VIOLET 400nm
👁️
Human Vision
Cone cells in the retina detect red, green, and blue wavelengths — our colour perception
📸
Photography
Cameras capture visible light on sensors to form images of the world around us
💡
LED Lighting
Energy-efficient light-emitting diodes produce specific visible wavelengths for illumination
💻
Fibre Optics
Laser light carries data through glass fibres — the backbone of modern internet infrastructure
The Spectrum — Part 5
🔆 Ultraviolet Radiation
Key Facts
• Wavelength: 10 nm – 400 nm
• Frequency just above visible violet
• Mostly blocked by Earth's ozone layer
• Can cause damage to living tissue (ionising)
• Three types: UV-A, UV-B, UV-C
UV-A
320–400nm
Tanning, ageing
UV-B
280–320nm
Sunburn, Vit D
UV-C
100–280nm
Germicidal
🦠
Sterilisation
UV-C light destroys DNA in bacteria and viruses — used in hospitals, water purification, and labs
💡
Fluorescence
UV causes certain materials to fluoresce (glow) — used in banknote security and forensics
☀️
Vitamin D
Skin uses UV-B radiation to synthesise vitamin D, essential for bone health and immunity
🔭
Astronomy
UV telescopes observe hot young stars, galaxies and active galactic nuclei that emit strongly in UV
The Spectrum — Part 6
🩻 X-Rays
Key Facts
• Wavelength: 0.01 nm – 10 nm
• Very high frequency, high energy
• Ionising radiation — can damage DNA
• Penetrate soft tissue but absorbed by bone/metal
• Discovered by Wilhelm Röntgen in 1895
λ : 0.01 – 10 nm  |  E = hf
⚠️ X-rays are ionising — prolonged exposure can break chemical bonds in DNA and cause cancer. Protective lead shields are used.
🦷
Medical X-rays
Image bones, detect fractures and cavities. Bones absorb X-rays, appearing white on film
🫁
CT Scans
Computed Tomography takes multiple X-ray images to build detailed 3D images of internal organs
🔐
Security Scanning
Airport and customs scanners use X-rays to inspect luggage and cargo for contraband
🌠
Astronomy
X-ray telescopes observe black holes, neutron stars and supernova remnants — sources of extreme energies
The Spectrum — Part 7
☢️ Gamma Rays
Key Facts
• Shortest wavelength (< 0.01 nm)
• Highest frequency and highest energy
• Emitted from radioactive nuclei during nuclear decay
• Strongly ionising — very penetrating, dangerous
• Thick lead or concrete needed for shielding
λ < 0.01 nm  |  f > 3 × 10¹⁹ Hz
🌌 Cosmic gamma-ray bursts are the most energetic explosions in the universe — some from neutron star mergers or supernovae
🎯
Cancer Treatment
Gamma Knife radiotherapy targets tumours with precise beams of gamma radiation to destroy cancer cells
🧫
Sterilisation
Medical equipment and food is sterilised using gamma irradiation — kills all microorganisms effectively
⚛️
PET Scans
Positron Emission Tomography: detects gamma rays to image metabolic activity in living tissue
🌠
Astrophysics
Gamma-ray telescopes (Fermi) detect gamma bursts from neutron stars, black holes and supernovae
Summary
Complete EM Spectrum at a Glance
Wave Type Wavelength Frequency Energy Key Uses
📻 Radio 0.1 m – 100 km 3 Hz – 3 GHz Lowest Broadcasting, MRI, mobile phones
📡 Microwave 1 mm – 10 cm 300 MHz – 300 GHz Low Cooking, radar, WiFi, GPS
🌡️ Infrared 700 nm – 1 mm 300 GHz – 430 THz Medium-low Heat, remote controls, thermal cameras
🌈 Visible 400 – 700 nm 430 – 750 THz Medium Vision, photography, fibre optics
🔆 Ultraviolet 10 – 400 nm 750 THz – 30 PHz Medium-high Sterilisation, fluorescence, Vit D
🩻 X-ray 0.01 – 10 nm 30 PHz – 30 EHz High Medical imaging, CT scans, security
☢️ Gamma < 0.01 nm > 30 EHz Highest Cancer therapy, sterilisation, PET scans
🔑 All EM waves travel at the speed of light (c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s) in a vacuum. They differ only in frequency and wavelength. Energy increases with frequency: E = hf.

Lesson Wrap-Up
What We Learned Today 🎓
  • ⚡ Electromagnetic radiation consists of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that travel at c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s in a vacuum — no medium required.
  • 〰️ Key wave relationship: c = f × λ and photon energy E = hf. Higher frequency means shorter wavelength and more energy.
  • 📊 The EM spectrum spans from radio waves (lowest energy, longest λ) to gamma rays (highest energy, shortest λ).
  • 📻 Radio waves — TV, FM/AM radio, MRI, mobile phones
  • 📡 Microwaves — Cooking, radar, WiFi, satellite communications
  • 🌡️ Infrared — Heat, thermal cameras, remote controls, IR astronomy
  • 🌈 Visible light — Human vision, photography, LED lighting, fibre optics
  • 🔆 Ultraviolet — Sterilisation, fluorescence, vitamin D synthesis (ionising)
  • 🩻 X-rays — Medical imaging, CT scans, airport security (strongly ionising)
  • ☢️ Gamma rays — Cancer radiotherapy, sterilisation, PET scans (most dangerous)
