4.6.2.2 Evolution
3
🌿 Evolution

What Is Evolution?

Evolution is a change in the inherited characteristics of a population over time through a process of natural selection, which may result in the formation of a new species.

The Big Idea

  • All species of living things have evolved from simple life forms
  • Life first developed more than 3 billion years ago
  • Over time, populations change as the environment changes
  • This is the theory of evolution by natural selection

New Species

  • If two populations of one species become very different in phenotype...
  • ...and can no longer interbreed to produce fertile offspring...
  • ...they have formed two new species
  • This process is called speciation

💡 Key phrase to remember: Evolution is a change in inherited characteristics — only changes in DNA (not learned behaviours) can be passed on and drive evolution.

4.6.2.2 Natural Selection
4
🌿 Natural Selection

How Natural Selection Works

🧬 Variation exists in population
→
🌍 Environment creates pressure (predation, disease, competition)
→
✅ Some variants are better suited — they survive
→
👶 Survivors reproduce and pass on favourable alleles
→
📈 Favourable trait becomes more common over generations

In Simple Terms

Natural selection is often summarised as "survival of the fittest" — but fittest means best adapted to the environment, not strongest or fastest!

Variants that give rise to better phenotypes

  • Arise through mutations
  • Are heritable — passed on in genes
  • Become more common over many generations
  • Can eventually lead to a new species
4.6.2.3 Selective Breeding
5
🌾 Selective Breeding

Selective Breeding

Selective breeding (artificial selection) is the process by which humans breed plants and animals for particular genetic characteristics. Humans have been doing this for thousands of years — since they first bred food crops from wild plants and domesticated animals.

How It Works

  • Choose parents with the desired characteristic from a mixed population
  • Breed them together
  • From offspring, select those with the best expression of the trait
  • Breed those together
  • Repeat over many generations until all offspring show the desired characteristic

Examples

  • 🌾 Disease resistance in food crops
  • 🐄 Animals that produce more meat or milk
  • 🐕 Domestic dogs with a gentle nature
  • 🌸 Large or unusual flowers

⚠ Risk: Inbreeding

  • Selective breeding reduces genetic diversity
  • Can cause inbreeding — some breeds become prone to disease or inherited defects
  • e.g. hip dysplasia in certain dog breeds
4.6.2.4 Genetic Engineering
6
🧬 Genetic Engineering

Genetic Engineering

Genetic engineering involves modifying the genome of an organism by introducing a gene from another organism to give a desired characteristic. Genes from humans and other organisms can be cut out and transferred to cells of other organisms.

Examples in Agriculture

  • 🌾 Crops engineered to be resistant to diseases
  • 🌾 Crops resistant to insect attack or herbicides
  • 🌾 GM crops show increased yields
  • 🍅 Produce bigger, better fruits

Examples in Medicine

  • 🦠 Bacteria engineered to produce human insulin (to treat diabetes)
  • 🧬 Research into overcoming inherited disorders

✅ Benefits

  • Crops resistant to disease and pests
  • Higher food yields → tackle food shortages
  • Cheap production of medicines like insulin
  • Potential to treat genetic disorders

⚠ Concerns

  • Effect on populations of wild flowers and insects
  • Long-term effects on human health not fully explored
  • Some people have ethical and moral objections
  • Remains highly controversial

4.6.3.1 Evidence for Evolution

🔍 Evidence for Evolution

Evidence for Evolution

The theory of evolution by natural selection is now widely accepted. Evidence comes from multiple sources.

🧬

Genes

It has been shown that characteristics are passed on to offspring in genes — this supports Darwin's idea that traits are inherited

🪨

Fossil Record

Fossils show how organisms have changed over millions of years — we can trace changes in body structure through time

🦠

Antibiotic Resistance

We can observe evolution happening in real time through bacteria developing resistance to antibiotics

💡 Darwin originally proposed his theory in 1859 — before genes were even discovered. The discovery of genetics and DNA later provided further strong support for his theory.

4.6.3.2 Fossils
9
🪨 Fossils

Fossils

Fossils are the remains of organisms from millions of years ago, found in rocks. They give us a window into how life has changed over time.

How Fossils Form

  • 🦴 From parts of organisms that did not decay (one or more conditions for decay were absent)
  • 🪨 When parts of the organism are replaced by minerals as they decay
  • 👣 As preserved traces — footprints, burrows, rootlet traces

What Fossils Tell Us

  • How much or how little organisms have changed over time
  • The sequence in which different organisms appeared on Earth
  • Relationships between extinct and living species

⚠ Why the Fossil Record is Incomplete

  • Many early life forms were soft-bodied → left few traces
  • Traces that did form were often destroyed by geological activity (heat, pressure, movement of tectonic plates)
  • Therefore scientists cannot be certain how life began on Earth

Evolutionary Trees

  • Scientists use evolutionary trees to show how organisms are related
  • Use current classification data for living organisms
  • Use fossil data for extinct organisms
  • You should be able to read and interpret evolutionary trees
4.6.3.3 Extinction
10
💀 Extinction

Extinction

Extinction occurs when there are no remaining individuals of a species still alive.

Factors That May Cause Extinction

  • 🌡 Climate change — the environment changes faster than a species can adapt
  • 🦠 New diseases — a pathogen the species has no resistance to
  • 🐅 New predators — introduced species that prey on the organism
  • 🌾 Loss of habitat — destruction of the ecosystem the species depends on
  • 🍽 Loss of food source — if prey or food plants disappear
  • 🏭 Human activity — hunting, pollution, deforestation
  • 🌋 Catastrophic events — asteroid impact, massive volcanic eruption

Key Point

A species becomes extinct when it can no longer successfully reproduce and its population drops to zero. Extinction is permanent — once a species is gone it is gone forever.

4.6.3.4 Resistant Bacteria
11
🦠 Antibiotic Resistance

Antibiotic Resistant Bacteria

How Resistance Develops

  • Bacteria reproduce rapidly → evolve fast
  • Mutations produce new strains
  • Some strains are resistant to antibiotics → not killed
  • Resistant bacteria survive and reproduce
  • Population of resistant strain rises
  • Resistant strain spreads — people are not immune and there is no effective treatment

Example: MRSA

MRSA (Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus) is resistant to many antibiotics — a serious problem in hospitals.

✅ How to Reduce Resistance

  • 🩺 Doctors should not prescribe antibiotics inappropriately (e.g. for viral infections or non-serious cases)
  • 💊 Patients must complete their full course of antibiotics — so all bacteria are killed and none survive to mutate
  • 🌾 Agricultural use of antibiotics should be restricted

The Challenge

Developing new antibiotics is costly and slow. It is unlikely to keep up with the emergence of new resistant strains.

4.6.3.4 Resistant Bacteria
13
🔗 Linking to Natural Selection

Resistance = Evolution in Action

Antibiotic resistance is a real-life example of natural selection happening very quickly:

🦠 Large population of bacteria — with natural variation
→
💊 Antibiotic introduced — acts as selection pressure
→
☠ Most bacteria killed — but a few have a resistance mutation
→
✅ Resistant bacteria survive and reproduce rapidly
→
📈 New resistant strain dominates — antibiotic no longer works

💡 This is exactly the same process as any other natural selection — variation → selection pressure → survival of fittest → reproduction → change in population. Bacteria just do it much faster because they reproduce so quickly!

🌍 This is why antibiotic resistance is a global health crisis — and why scientists are urgently working to develop new antibiotics and alternative treatments.

4.6.4 Classification

🔬 Classification

Classification — The Linnaean System

Carl Linnaeus

  • Developed a system of classifying living things based on structure and characteristics
  • Used a hierarchy of groups
  • Each organism given a two-part Latin name — the binomial system
  • Name = Genus + species (e.g. Homo sapiens)

Binomial System

  • Genus — always capitalised (e.g. Homo)
  • species — always lowercase (e.g. sapiens)
  • Written in italics (or underlined when handwritten)
  • Used universally by scientists worldwide

The Linnaean Hierarchy

Kingdom
Broadest group — e.g. Animalia
Phylum
e.g. Chordata
Class
e.g. Mammalia
Order
e.g. Primates
Family
e.g. Hominidae
Genus
e.g. Homo
species
sapiens ← most specific

4.6.4 Classification
15
🔬 Three-Domain System

The Three-Domain System

As microscopes improved and understanding of biochemical processes grew, scientists proposed new models of classification. Carl Woese developed the three-domain system using evidence from chemical analysis.

🌋

Archaea

Primitive bacteria — usually living in extreme environments (very hot, very salty, very acidic)

e.g. bacteria in hot springs, deep ocean vents

🦠

Bacteria

True bacteria — the more familiar group of bacteria

e.g. E. coli, Staphylococcus, Lactobacillus

🌿

Eukaryota

Includes protists, fungi, plants and animals — all organisms with a true nucleus

e.g. humans, oak trees, mushrooms, amoeba

💡 Why did classification change? New evidence from chemical analysis (comparing DNA and cell chemistry) showed that Archaea and Bacteria are more different from each other than either is from Eukaryota. Science evolves as new evidence emerges!

4.6.4 Evolutionary Trees
16
🌳 Evolutionary Trees

Evolutionary Trees

What Are They?

  • Diagrams used by scientists to show how organisms are related to one another
  • Show which species share a common ancestor
  • Use current classification data for living organisms
  • Use fossil data for extinct organisms

How to Read Them

  • A branch point (node) = a common ancestor
  • Organisms at the tips of branches = living or extinct species
  • The closer two organisms share a branch point, the more closely related they are
  • Longer branches = more evolutionary change

4.6.2.2 – 4.6.4 Summary

🌿 Evolution & Natural Selection

  • Evolution = change in inherited characteristics over time via natural selection
  • All life evolved from simple forms >3 billion years ago
  • New species form when populations can no longer interbreed
  • Variation → selection pressure → survival of fittest → reproduction → evolution

🌾 Selective Breeding vs Genetic Engineering

  • Selective breeding: choose parents, breed, select, repeat — over many generations
  • Genetic engineering: directly insert a gene from another organism
  • Both: reduce genetic diversity; carry ethical concerns
  • Both: have benefits in agriculture and medicine

🪨 Fossils & Extinction

  • Fossils = remains in rock; show change over millions of years
  • Record is incomplete — soft bodies, geological destruction
  • Extinction = no individuals left; permanent
  • Caused by: climate change, disease, predators, habitat loss, catastrophes

🦠 Antibiotic Resistance

  • Bacteria evolve fast — rapid reproduction = rapid evolution
  • Mutation → resistant strain → survives antibiotics → reproduces → spreads
  • MRSA = key example
  • Reduce resistance: don't prescribe unnecessarily; complete full course; restrict agricultural use
  • New antibiotic development is slow and costly

🔬 Classification

  • Linnaeus: Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → species
  • Binomial system: Genus species (e.g. Homo sapiens)
  • Three-domain system (Woese): Archaea, Bacteria, Eukaryota
  • Based on chemical/biochemical evidence
  • Evolutionary trees show relationships and common ancestors
