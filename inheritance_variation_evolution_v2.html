🔬 Reproduction

Sexual vs Asexual Reproduction

Feature 🌸 Sexual Reproduction 🌱 Asexual Reproduction
Parents neededTwo (male & female)One
Gametes involved?Yes — sperm + egg (animals); pollen + egg (plants)No
Fusion of gametes?YesNo
Genetic mixing?Yes → variety in offspringNo → genetically identical (clones)
Cell division typeMeiosis (to form gametes)Mitosis only
Offspring typeNon-identicalClones

💡 Key idea: Sexual reproduction introduces genetic variation which is the raw material for evolution. Asexual reproduction is faster but produces no variety.

4.6.1.2 Meiosis
4
🔬 Meiosis

Meiosis — Forming Gametes

What happens in Meiosis?

  • Copies of genetic information are first made
  • Cell divides twice → produces 4 gametes
  • Each gamete has a single set of chromosomes (haploid)
  • All gametes are genetically different from each other

After Fertilisation

  • Two gametes fuse → normal chromosome number restored (diploid)
  • New cell divides by mitosis → cell number increases
  • As embryo develops → cells differentiate
4.6.1.3 DNA & Genome
5
🧬 DNA Structure

DNA and the Genome

DNA Structure

  • DNA is a polymer made of two strands
  • Forms a double helix shape
  • Contained in structures called chromosomes
  • Found in the nucleus of a cell

Genes

  • A gene = a small section of DNA on a chromosome
  • Each gene codes for a specific sequence of amino acids
  • This makes a specific protein

The Genome

  • The genome = the entire genetic material of an organism
  • The human genome has now been fully sequenced
  • Important for: finding genes linked to disease, treating inherited disorders, tracing human migration

Genetic Inheritance — Key Terms

GameteSex cell (sperm, egg, pollen)
ChromosomeStructure in nucleus carrying genes
GeneSection of DNA coding for a protein
AlleleDifferent form of a gene
DominantAlways expressed — even if only one copy present
RecessiveOnly expressed if TWO copies present
HomozygousBoth alleles are the same (AA or aa)
HeterozygousAlleles are different (Aa)
GenotypeThe alleles present (e.g. Aa, BB, bb)
PhenotypeThe observable characteristic (e.g. brown eyes)

💡 Remember: Most characteristics result from multiple genes interacting, not just one gene. Single-gene inheritance (like fur colour in mice, red-green colour blindness) is used to teach the principles.

4.6.1.5 Inherited Disorders
8
🧬 Inherited Disorders

Inherited Disorders

🖐 Polydactyly

  • Having extra fingers or toes
  • Caused by a dominant allele
  • Only ONE copy needed to show the disorder
  • An affected parent with Pp has 50% chance of passing it on

💨 Cystic Fibrosis

  • A disorder of cell membranes
  • Caused by a recessive allele
  • TWO copies needed to be affected (ff)
  • Carriers (Ff) are unaffected but can pass it on

⚖ Embryo Screening — Ethical Considerations

Benefits:

  • Can prevent suffering
  • May alleviate inherited disorders
  • Parents can make informed choices

Ethical concerns:

  • Who decides which embryos are "acceptable"?
  • Could lead to "designer babies"
  • Discarding embryos raises moral questions

4.6.2.1 Variation

🌿 Variation

Variation

Differences in characteristics among individuals in a population is called variation. It may be due to:

🧬

Genetic

Differences in the genes inherited from parents

e.g. eye colour, blood type

🌍

Environmental

Conditions in which the organism developed

e.g. language spoken, scars, diet effects

🔀

Both

Combination of genes and environment

e.g. height, skin colour, intelligence

Mutations and Variation

  • All variants arise from mutations — changes in DNA
  • Most mutations → no effect on phenotype
  • Some mutations → influence the phenotype
  • Very few mutations → determine the phenotype
  • Mutations occur continuously
4.6.2 Evolution
12
🌿 Natural Selection

Natural Selection & Evolution

How Natural Selection Works

  • Variation exists in a population
  • Some individuals have advantageous traits
  • These individuals are more likely to survive and reproduce
  • They pass on their beneficial alleles to offspring
  • Over generations → the trait becomes more common

Mutations and New Phenotypes

  • Mutations occur continuously
  • Very rarely a mutation → new phenotype
  • If suited to environmental change → rapid change in species
  • This is how evolution happens!
4.6 Selective Breeding

🐄 Selective Breeding

Selective Breeding

Scientists use understanding of inheritance to selectively breed livestock and plants with favoured characteristics.

How It Works

  • Choose parents with the desired trait
  • Breed them together
  • Select offspring with the best expression of the trait
  • Repeat over many generations

Examples

  • 🐄 Cattle with high milk yield
  • 🌾 Wheat with disease resistance
  • 🐕 Dogs bred for specific temperament
  • 🌸 Flowers with larger, brighter blooms

⚠ Concerns

  • Reduced genetic diversity → whole population vulnerable to new disease
  • Inbreeding can introduce harmful recessive alleles
  • Animal welfare concerns
  • Loss of natural traits

💡 Compare with natural selection: selective breeding is directed by humans, natural selection is directed by the environment.

4.6 Cloning
14
🔬 Cloning

Cloning

Once new varieties of plants or animals are produced, cloning can produce large numbers of identical individuals — all carrying the favourable characteristic.

🌱 Plant Cloning

  • Cuttings — take a stem/leaf and grow a new plant
  • Tissue culture — small piece of plant tissue grown in sterile nutrient medium
  • Fast and inexpensive
  • Produces large numbers of identical plants

🐑 Animal Cloning

  • Embryo transplants — split embryo early on, implant in surrogate mothers
  • Nuclear transfer (e.g. Dolly the sheep) — transfer nucleus from adult cell into enucleated egg
  • Much more difficult than plant cloning

✅ Benefits

  • Preserve endangered species
  • Mass produce organisms with useful traits
  • Medical research

⚠ Concerns

  • Reduces genetic diversity
  • Ethical issues in animal cloning
  • Cloned animals may age faster
4.6 Genetic Engineering

🧬 Genetic Engineering

Genetic Engineering

Scientists can take a gene from one species and introduce it into the genome of another. This is called genetic engineering (or genetic modification / GM).

How It Works (General)

  • Identify the desired gene in the donor organism
  • The gene is removed and inserted into the recipient organism's DNA
  • The organism now expresses the new gene — producing the desired characteristic

Examples

  • 🩺 Bacteria engineered to produce human insulin
  • 🌾 GM crops resistant to pests or herbicides
  • Golden Rice — produces beta-carotene (vitamin A)

✅ Potential Benefits

  • Treat or cure genetic diseases
  • Produce medicines cheaply
  • Improve crop yields and nutrition
  • Adapt crops to climate change

⚠ Controversy

  • Unknown long-term ecological effects
  • Ethical concerns about "playing God"
  • Corporate control over food supply
  • Possible allergen introduction
  • Remains highly controversial despite potential

📋 Topic Summary

Topic 4.6 — Key Points Summary

🔬 Reproduction & Meiosis

  • Sexual reproduction → variety (meiosis + fertilisation)
  • Asexual reproduction → clones (mitosis only)
  • Meiosis: 1 cell → 4 genetically different gametes (haploid)
  • Fertilisation restores the diploid number

🧬 DNA & Inheritance

  • DNA → double helix polymer → in chromosomes
  • Gene = section of DNA → codes for one protein
  • Genome = ALL genetic material of an organism
  • Dominant allele always expressed; recessive needs two copies
  • Punnett squares → read and interpret genotype/phenotype outcomes

🧬 Inherited Disorders

  • Polydactyly → dominant allele
  • Cystic fibrosis → recessive allele
  • Embryo screening → ethical debate
  • Sex: XX = female; XY = male; 50:50 ratio

🌿 Variation & Evolution

  • Variation: genetic, environmental, or both
  • All variants arise from mutations
  • Mutations are continuous but mostly have no phenotype effect
  • Natural selection → beneficial traits increase in population → evolution

⚗ Human Intervention

  • Selective breeding → choose parents with desired traits
  • Cloning → large numbers of genetically identical individuals
  • Genetic engineering → insert gene from one species into another
  • All carry benefits AND ethical/ecological concerns
