Part One
Common
Vocabulary
Before we draw a single ray, let's make sure we all understand the same language. Lenses and mirrors share the same set of terms.
Key Terms

Principal Axis

The horizontal reference line that passes through the centre of the lens or mirror. All measurements are made relative to this line.

Principal Plane

A vertical reference line passing through the exact centre of the lens — or perpendicular to the axis at the centre of a mirror.

Focal Point (F)

The point on the principal axis where parallel rays of light converge (or appear to diverge from) after passing through the lens or reflecting off the mirror.

Object

The item we are examining. In ray diagrams it is always drawn as a vertical upright arrow on the principal axis, on the left side of the lens or mirror.

Part Two
Types of Lenses
& Mirrors
There are two types of each. What makes them different? The shape — and how they handle light.
Types of Lenses

Convex Lens

(Converging Lens)
Thickest in the middle. Bends parallel rays inward so they converge at the focal point.

Concave Lens

(Diverging Lens)
Thinnest in the middle. Spreads parallel rays outward. Light appears to come from a virtual focal point.

Remember: Ray diagrams in this course are for converging (convex) lenses only. The concave lens is for understanding only.
Types of Mirrors

Concave Mirror

(Converging Mirror)
Curves inward — like the inside of a bowl. Parallel rays reflect and converge at the focal point.

Convex Mirror

(Diverging Mirror)
Curves outward — like the outside of a ball. Parallel rays reflect and spread out. Virtual focal point behind mirror.

Remember: Ray diagrams in this course are for concave (converging) mirrors only. Notice: a converging lens is convex, but a converging mirror is concave — both bend light to a focal point.
Part Three
Ray Tracing
There are infinitely many light rays from any object. We use just three special rays to find where the image forms. Each ray starts from the tip of the object.
The Three Rays
These same three rules apply to both converging lenses and concave mirrors. Always draw from the tip of the object. Where the three rays cross — that is your image.
①

Parallel Ray: Draw a ray from the object tip, parallel to the principal axis. When it hits the lens/mirror, it bends (refracts or reflects) and passes through the far focal point F.

②

Centre Ray: Draw a ray from the object tip, through the centre of the lens (or toward the centre of curvature C for mirrors). This ray passes straight through without changing direction.

③

Focal Ray: Draw a ray from the object tip, through the near focal point F. After passing through the lens/reflecting off the mirror, it exits parallel to the principal axis.

The point where all three rays intersect is where the image forms. The characteristics of that image (real or virtual, upright or inverted, larger or smaller) depend on where they intersect relative to the principal axis.

Converging Lens — Ray Diagram
Object placed beyond the focal point

① Parallel Ray → through far F

Travels horizontal, bends at lens, exits through the far focal point

② Centre Ray → straight through

Passes through the centre of the lens — direction unchanged

③ Focal Ray → exits parallel

Aims through near focal point, exits parallel to principal axis

📍 Image formed

Real, inverted, same size (object at 2F). On opposite side of lens.

Concave Mirror — Ray Diagram
Object placed beyond the focal point

① Parallel → reflects through F

Travels parallel, reflects off mirror, passes through focal point F

② Through F → reflects parallel

Aimed through F, reflects off mirror, exits parallel to axis

③ Through C → reflects back

Aimed through C, hits mirror perpendicularly, reflects straight back through C

📍 Image formed

Real, inverted, smaller. On same side as object (between F and C).

Part Four
Image
Characteristics
Once you have drawn your ray diagram, you need to describe the image. Every image has three characteristics to identify.
Describing the Image
1 · Real or Virtual?

Real Image

  • Formed where rays actually cross
  • Can be projected onto a screen
  • Lens: on the opposite side from the object
  • Mirror: on the same side as the object

Virtual Image

  • Rays do not actually cross — our eyes trace them back
  • Cannot be projected onto a screen
  • Lens: on the same side as the object
  • Mirror: on the opposite side from the object
2 · Upright or Inverted?
↑

Upright

Rays intersect above the principal axis. Image points the same way as the object.

↓

Inverted

Rays intersect below the principal axis. Image is flipped upside-down compared to the object.

3 · Image Size
🔍

Larger

Image is bigger than the object — magnified

⬜

Same Size

Image is equal in size to the object

🔎

Smaller

Image is smaller than the object — diminished

Understanding Virtual Images
A common misconception is that virtual images look blurry or altered. That is not true. The key difference between a real and virtual image is simply whether the image can be projected on a screen. Virtual images look perfectly normal to the eye.
🔬

Magnifying Glass

A convex lens used as a magnifying glass forms a virtual image. The image is larger, upright, and visible through the lens — but only because you are looking back through it. It cannot be projected onto a wall.

🪞

Flat (Plane) Mirror

A flat mirror always forms a virtual image. The image appears to be behind the mirror — same size as the object, upright, and perfectly clear. But if you put a screen behind the mirror, nothing would appear on it.

The Rule to Remember

A real image is formed by rays that actually cross. You can project it on a screen — like a movie projector. A virtual image is formed by rays that appear to cross only because your brain traces the diverging rays backward. You can see it, but you cannot project it.

How to Use It

You will always be given two of the three variables and asked to find the third. Rearrange the equation and solve. The last step is always to take the reciprocal of your answer to find the unknown distance.

Example Problem — Lens
Question: A candle flame is placed 25.0 cm from a double convex lens. The focal length of the lens is 18.4 cm. Where is the image of the candle flame?

④

Interpreting the Answer

dᵢ = +69.7 cm. The positive value tells us the image is on the opposite side of the lens from the object — a real image. It is further from the lens than the object, so it is also larger than the candle flame.
